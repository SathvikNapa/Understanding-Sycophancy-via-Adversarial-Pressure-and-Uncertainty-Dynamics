\paragraph{HLE stress test reveals a third behavioral mode: fragmentation.}
The HLE results (GPT-5.4 and Gemini 3.5 Flash; remaining four cells deferred per Table~\ref{tab:coverage}) extend the resister/capitulator taxonomy from the two easier benchmarks. Cumulative $T_1$--$T_6$ flip rates on HLE are $22.6\%$ (GPT-5.4) and $28.8\%$ (Gemini), both lower than the MMLU-Pro/GPQA-Diamond ranges for the same models, an artifact of HLE's broadly non-degenerate baseline uncertainty, which leaves the certain-vs.-uncertain contrast that drove flip rates on MMLU-Pro largely undefined here. The more informative HLE signal is consensus loss: at $5$ CoT samples per turn we record the turn at which no single answer wins a majority. On HLE, Gemini 3.5 Flash loses consensus on $72.2\%$ of $T_0$-correct queries, with the first loss occurring on average at turn $1.81$, whereas GPT-5.4 loses consensus on only $21.4\%$ of queries and on average not until turn $4.33$. This is a third behavioral mode distinct from both the resisters and the capitulators of Table~\ref{tab:rq3}: the model neither defends the correct answer nor commits to the planted wrong answer, but instead fragments into incoherent self-disagreement under pressure. We refer to this mode as \emph{fragmenter} and discuss its implications for deployment in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\paragraph{Single-turn baseline confirms the uncertainty conditioning.} To rule out the possibility that the uncertainty effect of RQ1 and the pressure-category ordering of RQ3 are specific to multi-turn dynamics, we ran a single-turn pressure variant on MMLU-Pro: for each (model, question, pressure category) cell, we issue exactly one pressure prompt after the baseline answer and record whether the model flips. Two findings from the multi-turn analyses survive stripping away accumulated pressure. First, the relative ordering of pressure categories is remarkably stable across models: expert authority is the single most-flipping category for every one of the six included models, followed by social proof and personal disagreement; personal certainty and crowd consensus are consistently the least effective (Appendix~\ref{app:single_turn} reports the full per-category rates). Second, the certain-vs.-uncertain gap of RQ1 persists in the single-turn regime: uncertain-baseline questions flip $30$--$80\%$ more often than certain-baseline ones, with Spearman $\rho$ between baseline entropy $H$ and single-turn flip rate in the range $[-0.39, -0.05]$ per model (significant at $p{<}0.05$ for Claude Haiku, GPT-5.4, and GPT-5.4 Mini). This confirms that uncertainty conditioning is a property of the target model's response to \emph{any} social pressure, not an emergent effect of accumulated multi-turn dynamics.
