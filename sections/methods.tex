\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

We describe the four components of our evaluation framework: (i) the uncertainty estimator, (ii) the multi-turn pressure protocol, (iii) the flip definition, and (iv) the external calibrator used for chain-of-thought analysis. All four components are black-box: they require only API access and assume nothing about model weights, logits, or internal states.

\subsection{Uncertainty Signals}
\label{sec:methods:signals}

Three distinct uncertainty signals underpin our analyses, each operating at a different temporal and analytical granularity. We introduce them together here as a reference; formal definitions and usage details are deferred to the subsections cross-referenced below.

\paragraph{(1) Pre-pressure behavioral uncertainty $H(q;\mathcal{M})$.}
Shannon entropy over $K = 25$ independent answer samples drawn from the target model at temperature 1, conditioned only on the question $q$ (no adversarial context). $H(q;\mathcal{M}) = 0$ when all samples agree (the model is behaviorally certain) and $H(q;\mathcal{M}) < 0$ when samples spread across multiple options. This is the headline uncertainty signal of the paper: it characterizes a model's belief state on $q$ \emph{before} any pressure begins, and serves as the regressor in RQ1, RQ2, and RQ3. Defined formally in

Section~\ref{sec:methods:entropy}.

\paragraph{(2) Per-turn belief entropy $B_t(q;\mathcal{M})$.}
Shannon entropy over the $R = 5$ majority beliefs obtained from running $R$ independent multi-turn dialogues at pressure turn $t$, where the majority belief in each dialogue is the modal answer across $K_{\text{turn}} = 5$ re-polls within that dialogue. $B_t$ captures the spread of the model's response across independent adversarial conversations \emph{at a given turn under pressure}, and appears as the \emph{Bel.\ Ent.} column of the RQ4 table
(Appendix~\ref{app:rq4}). $B_t$ and $H$ are distinct measurements: $H$ measures spread before any pressure with no dialogue context, while $B_t$ measures spread during pressure across replicate dialogues. Defined in Section~\ref{sec:methods:pressure}.

\paragraph{(3) Step-level calibrated confidence $\hat{\gamma}_\ell$.}
Verbalized confidence $\hat{\gamma}_\ell \in [0, 100]$ assigned by the external calibrator $\mathcal{C}$ at step $\ell$ of a free-form reasoning trace, alongside an inferred belief $\hat{c}_\ell$ over the option set $\mathcal{A}$. Unlike $H$ and $B_t$, which are black-box behavioral measures derived from repeated sampling on the target model, $\hat{\gamma}_\ell$ is a verbalized scalar that approximates how confident the partial reasoning \emph{appears} to an external reader. Defined in Section~\ref{sec:methods:calibrator} and validated against ground-truth correctness in

Appendix~\ref{app:calibration}.

\paragraph{(4) Calibrated hardness $\hat{h}(q;\mathcal{M},\mathcal{D})$.}
A complementary view of pre-pressure uncertainty: instead of using the raw entropy $H(q;\mathcal{M})$, we map $|H|$ to a per-(model, dataset) calibrated probability that the model answers $q$
incorrectly on a fresh attempt. The mapping is fit by isotonic regression on baseline data, yielding $\hat{h} \in [0, 1]$ on the same scale as expected error rate. Defined in
Section~\ref{sec:methods:hardness}; this signal is used as an alternative to $H$ in supplementary regressions and to construct the Easy/Medium/Hard bins of Appendix~\ref{app:entropy_dist}.

The four signals are complementary. $H$ partitions the question pool into a stable certain group ($H = 0$) and a volatile uncertain group ($H < 0$); $\hat{h}$ projects that partition onto a calibrated error-probability scale for cross-cell comparability; $B_t$ tracks how the volatility evolves turn by turn under sustained pressure; and $\hat{\gamma}_\ell$ provides a step-level signal inside a single reasoning trace that exposes \emph{where in the reasoning} a flip is signaled. Together they support measurement at three resolutions: \emph{per question} (via $H$ and $\hat{h}$), \emph{per turn} (via $B_t$), and \emph{per reasoning step} (via $\hat{\gamma}_\ell$).

\subsection{Behavioral Uncertainty via Repeated Sampling}
\label{sec:methods:entropy}

Let $\mathcal{M}$ denote a target model and $q$ a multiple-choice question
with option set $\mathcal{A} = \{A, B, \ldots, J\}$ and gold answer
$a^* \in \mathcal{A}$. We estimate the model's pre-pressure belief
distribution by sampling $K = 25$ independent answers from $\mathcal{M}$
at temperature~$1$, conditioned only on the question (no adversarial
turns):
\[
S_q = \{a^{(1)}, a^{(2)}, \ldots, a^{(K)}\}, \qquad a^{(k)} \sim \mathcal{M}(\cdot \mid q).
\]
The empirical distribution over options is $\hat{p}_q(a) = \tfrac{1}{K}\sum_k \mathbf{1}[a^{(k)} = a]$,
and we define our entropy signal as
\[
H(q; \mathcal{M}) = \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \hat{p}_q(a)\,\log \hat{p}_q(a).
\]
This is the negative of standard Shannon entropy, so that
$H(q;\mathcal{M}) = 0$ when all $K$ samples agree (the model is
behaviorally certain) and $H(q;\mathcal{M}) < 0$ when samples spread
across multiple options (more negative = more spread). We use this
sign convention throughout so that the certain group ($H = 0$) and
the uncertain group ($H < 0$) of Section~\ref{sec:results} are
defined consistently. We use the natural logarithm throughout, and
treat $H(q;\mathcal{M})$ as a black-box proxy for the model's
epistemic state on question $q$ before any adversarial interaction.

\paragraph{Relation to semantic entropy.}
\citet{kuhn2023semanticuncertaintylinguisticinvariances} introduce
semantic entropy for open-ended generation, where surface variation
between samples (e.g.\ paraphrases of the same answer) inflates token-level
entropy estimates. Their solution is to cluster samples by semantic
equivalence using an entailment model before computing entropy over
clusters. In our setting, answers are discrete option letters $A$--$J$
with no surface variation: two samples either denote the same option or
denote different options. The semantic clustering step is therefore an
identity operation, and Shannon entropy over the empirical option
distribution is equivalent to semantic entropy in this regime. We adopt
this simpler estimator and refer to it as \textbf{behavioral uncertainty}
to distinguish it from token-level confidence measures.

\paragraph{Why $K = 25$.}
With $K = 25$, the minimum resolvable change in $\hat{p}_q$ is $0.04$,
sufficient to distinguish unanimous ($H = 0$) from near-unanimous
($\hat{p} = 24/25$, $H \approx 0.16$) responses while keeping per-question
sampling cost tractable across our included model families.

\subsection{Calibrated Hardness via Isotonic Regression}
\label{sec:methods:hardness}

The raw entropy $H(q;\mathcal{M})$ is a behavioral signal whose
absolute magnitude is not directly comparable across (model, dataset)
cells: a value of $H = -0.5$ may correspond to a very different
expected error rate for one model on MMLU-Pro than for another model
on GPQA-Diamond. To obtain a per-cell-comparable scalar, we project
$|H|$ onto the empirical baseline error rate via isotonic regression.

For each (model, dataset) cell $(\mathcal{M}, \mathcal{D})$ we
collect, at baseline ($T_0$), the empirical error indicator
$y_q = \mathbf{1}[\hat{a}_{q,0} \neq a^*_q]$ across all questions
$q \in \mathcal{D}$. We fit a non-decreasing function
$\phi_{\mathcal{M},\mathcal{D}}: \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \to [0, 1]$ that
minimizes the squared error
$\sum_q \big(y_q - \phi_{\mathcal{M},\mathcal{D}}(|H(q;\mathcal{M})|)\big)^2$
subject to monotonicity, using the standard pool-adjacent-violators
algorithm. The \emph{calibrated hardness} of a question is then
\[
\hat{h}(q;\mathcal{M},\mathcal{D}) = \phi_{\mathcal{M},\mathcal{D}}\!\bigl(|H(q;\mathcal{M})|\bigr) \in [0, 1],
\]
interpretable as the calibrated probability that $\mathcal{M}$ will
err on $q$ on a fresh sample, conditional on the observed baseline
entropy. By construction $\hat{h} = 0$ means the model is expected
to answer correctly with certainty and $\hat{h} = 1$ means expected
to err. The mapping $\phi_{\mathcal{M},\mathcal{D}}$ is fit per cell
and the same question can therefore have different hardness under
different (model, dataset) cells.

\paragraph{Hardness bins.} For partitioned analyses we discretize
$\hat{h}$ into three fixed-cut bins:
\textbf{Easy} ($\hat{h} \in [0, \tfrac{1}{3})$),
\textbf{Medium} ($\hat{h} \in [\tfrac{1}{3}, \tfrac{2}{3})$), and
\textbf{Hard} ($\hat{h} \in [\tfrac{2}{3}, 1]$). Empirically, the
isotonic fit is heavily quantized in many cells, with $>50\%$ of
questions mapping to a single $\hat{h}$ value; the resulting bin
distributions are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:entropy_dist}.

\subsection{Multi-Turn Pressure Protocol}
\label{sec:methods:pressure}

For each question $q$ we run a six-turn adversarial dialogue. After the
target model produces an initial answer (turn $T_0$), an adversary
asserts a specific wrong answer $a^\dagger \neq a^*$ across turns
$T_1$--$T_6$ using a fixed escalation sequence of rhetorical strategies.
The wrong answer $a^\dagger$ is held constant within a dialogue and is
drawn uniformly from $\mathcal{A} \setminus \{a^*\}$. The six pressure
levels (Table~\ref{tab:pressure_levels}) escalate from low-authority
personal pushback to high-authority external claims and finally to
crowd-consensus framing.

\paragraph{Rationale for escalation order.}
The six prompts span the rhetorical strategies most commonly identified
in human persuasion research and in prior sycophancy benchmarks
\citep{liu2025truthdecayquantifyingmultiturn,hong2025syconbench}:
personal pushback (no authority claim) $\rightarrow$ first-person certainty
$\rightarrow$ external evidence appeal $\rightarrow$ expert authority
$\rightarrow$ accusatory framing $\rightarrow$ universal consensus. Ordering
from low- to high-authority lets us measure both \textbf{whether} a model
flips and \textbf{which rhetorical category} is sufficient to cause the
first flip, while keeping the sequence comparable across models and
questions. The fixed ordering trades adaptive adversary strength for
controlled comparison; an adaptive adversary is left to future work.

\paragraph{Repeated dialogues for per-turn uncertainty.}
For each question we run $R = 5$ independent dialogues with different
random seeds, and at every turn $t \in \{T_0, \ldots, T_6\}$ we re-poll
the model $K_{\text{turn}} = 5$ times. This yields $R \cdot K_{\text{turn}} = 25$
samples per (question, turn) cell, matching the resolution used for the
pre-pressure estimate in Section~\ref{sec:methods:entropy}.

\paragraph{Per-turn belief entropy $B_t$.}
Within dialogue $r \in \{1, \ldots, R\}$ at turn $t$, let
$\hat{a}_{q,t,r} = \arg\max_a \hat{p}_{q,t,r}(a)$ be the modal answer
across the $K_{\text{turn}}$ re-polls of that dialogue (its
\emph{majority belief}). Let
$\hat{\pi}_{q,t}(a) = \tfrac{1}{R}\sum_{r=1}^{R}
\mathbf{1}[\hat{a}_{q,t,r} = a]$
denote the empirical distribution of majority beliefs across the $R$
dialogues. The per-turn belief entropy is
\[
B_t(q;\mathcal{M}) = \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \hat{\pi}_{q,t}(a)\,\log \hat{\pi}_{q,t}(a),
\]
using the same sign convention as $H$ in
Section~\ref{sec:methods:entropy}: $B_t = 0$ when all $R$ dialogues
land on the same majority answer at turn $t$, and $B_t < 0$ when the
$R$ dialogues disagree (more negative = more spread across independent
dialogues). $B_t$ is the \emph{Bel.\ Ent.} column reported per
(model, dataset, turn) cell in Table~\ref{tab:rq4} and is the
operational definition of \emph{consensus loss} for the fragmenter
analysis: a query is said to \emph{lose consensus} at turn $t$ when
no answer wins a majority of the $R$ dialogues, equivalently when
$\hat{\pi}_{q,t}$ has no mode at $\geq \lceil R/2 \rceil = 3$
dialogues.

\subsection{Flip Definition}
\label{sec:methods:flip}

At each turn $t$ we compute the majority answer
$\hat{a}_t = \arg\max_a \hat{p}_{q,t}(a)$ from the 5 repeated polls within
a single dialogue. A dialogue \textbf{flips} at turn $t$ if
\[
\hat{a}_{t-1} = a^* \quad \text{and} \quad \hat{a}_t = a^\dagger.
\]
The \textbf{turn-of-flip} (ToF) is the smallest such $t$; a dialogue that
never satisfies the condition through $T_6$ is recorded as
ToF~$=$~$\varnothing$. To avoid conflating sycophancy with baseline error,
all analyses are restricted to questions for which the model answered
correctly at $T_0$ (majority $\hat{a}_0 = a^*$ in at least four of five
runs); wrong-from-start questions are excluded throughout.

\subsection{Statistical Models}
\label{sec:methods:stats}

We use two regression specifications throughout the analyses, both
fit by maximum likelihood with a logit link on the binary flip
outcome.

\paragraph{Per-model logistic regression.}
For each (model, dataset) cell we fit a logistic regression of the
per-turn flip outcome on pressure level $P \in \{1, \ldots, 6\}$
(the turn index from Section~\ref{sec:methods:pressure}), baseline
entropy $H = H(q;\mathcal{M}) \in (-\infty, 0]$ (from
Section~\ref{sec:methods:entropy}), and their interaction $P H_c$.
Because the baseline entropy distribution differs substantially
across (model, dataset) cells --- for some models the vast majority
of questions have $H = 0$, for others every question has $H < 0$ ---
we mean-center $H$ within each cell before fitting:
\[
H_c \;=\; H \,-\, \bar{H}_{\text{model, dataset}},
\quad\text{so that}\quad \mathbb{E}[H_c] = 0.
\]
The fitted specification is then
\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{logit}\,\Pr(\mathrm{flip}_{q,t}) ={}& \beta_0 + \beta_P\,P_t \\
  & {} + \beta_H\,H_{c,q} + \beta_{PH}\,P_t\,H_{c,q}.
\end{aligned}
\]
Mean-centering makes $\beta_P$ interpretable as the marginal effect
of pressure at the \emph{typical} uncertainty level for that cell
rather than at the arbitrary reference point $H = 0$: for cells
where $H = 0$ is atypical (e.g.\ Claude Haiku on MMLU-Pro, where
$\bar H = -3.21$), the raw-scale $\hat\beta_P$ can have the
opposite sign from the empirical flip-rate slope reported in
\autoref{tab:rq3} because it is extrapolated to an unrepresentative
subset. Centering resolves this without changing the slope of $\beta_H$
or the interaction $\beta_{PH}$, which are both shift-invariant.
The coefficient $\beta_P$ is the pressure slope at $H_c = 0$:
$\beta_P < 0$ identifies a \emph{resister} (flip probability falls
as pressure accumulates), $\beta_P > 0$ a \emph{capitulator}. The
coefficient $\beta_H$ is the uncertainty effect ($\beta_H < 0$ means
more negative $H$, i.e.\ higher uncertainty, raises flip
probability). The interaction $\beta_{PH}$ tests whether pressure
amplifies pre-existing uncertainty: $\beta_{PH} > 0$ indicates that
each additional pressure turn raises flip probability more on
uncertain questions than on certain ones. Per-model coefficients
with $95\%$ confidence intervals are visualized as a forest plot in
\autoref{fig:rq2_coefs}; raw numerical estimates are tabulated in
Appendix~\ref{app:rq2_table}.

\paragraph{Mixed-effects extension.}
To test whether the pressure-by-entropy interaction observed in the
per-model regressions survives pooling, we additionally fit a
generalized linear mixed model with binomial link, the same fixed
effects, and a model-level random intercept
$u_m \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_u^2)$:
\[
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{logit}\,\Pr(\mathrm{flip}_{m,q,t}) ={}& \beta_0 + u_m + \beta_P\,P_t \\
  & {} + \beta_H\,H_{c,m,q} + \beta_{PH}\,P_t\,H_{c,m,q},
\end{aligned}
\]
using the same per-cell mean-centered entropy $H_c$ as the
per-model specification above.
The random intercept absorbs overall susceptibility differences
across models, so a significant fixed-effect $\beta_{PH}$ in this
specification cannot be explained by between-model differences in
baseline flip rate. The mixed-effects fit serves as a robustness
check for the headline interaction; the full output is reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:mixed_effects}.

\paragraph{Restriction to correct-at-$T_0$.}
Both regressions are fit only on dialogues whose target model
answered correctly at $T_0$ (majority $\hat{a}_0 = a^*$ in at least
four of five runs), consistent with the Flip Definition above. This
restriction ensures that the flip event is well-defined: a model
that begins wrong cannot ``flip from correct to wrong.'' The
correct-at-$T_0$ count differs per (model, dataset) cell, which is
the source of the varying $n$ reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:rq2_table}.

\subsection{External Calibrator for Reasoning Traces}
\label{sec:methods:calibrator}

For RQ4 we extend the framework to chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning.
The target model is prompted to reason step by step but is instructed
\textbf{not} to emit inline confidence or current-belief markers; the
output is a free-form reasoning trace $\tau = (z_1, \ldots, z_L)$
terminating in a single \texttt{FINAL ANSWER: <letter>} line, where each
$z_\ell$ is one reasoning step. We deliberately suppress inline
self-reported confidence because prior work shows that reasoning models
often produce confident but wrong self-assessments
\citep{mei2025reasoninguncertainty,yoon2025reasoningconfidence}; for
our purposes the relevant signal is what the trace \textbf{appears} to
support to an external reader, not what the target asserts about
itself.

A separate calibrator model $\mathcal{C}$ reads each prefix of the trace
and assigns a belief and confidence:
\[
(\hat{c}_\ell,\, \hat{\gamma}_\ell) = \mathcal{C}\!\left(q,\, z_1,\ldots, z_\ell\right),
\]
where $\hat{c}_\ell \in \mathcal{A}$ is the option the partial reasoning
appears to support and $\hat{\gamma}_\ell \in [0, 100]$ is its expressed
verbalized confidence \citep{tian2023justask}. The calibrator is
strictly incremental: when evaluating step $\ell$ it sees only
$z_1, \ldots, z_\ell$ and never future steps, so its output reflects the
evidential state of the argument as it unfolds rather than a
retrospective label. This prefix-only setup follows the partial-trace
success-probability framing used by \citet{park2025knowwhatyoudontknow}
for process reward models, but differs in two ways: we treat the
prefix-conditioned signal as a measurement of the target's belief
trajectory rather than as a stopping rule for best-of-$N$ search, and
we do not train $\mathcal{C}$ to be a calibrated estimator: we use an
off-the-shelf LLM and validate it post hoc (below). The setup is also
related to introspective uncertainty quantification
\citep{mei2025reasoninguncertainty}, with two distinctions:
$\mathcal{C}$ is a \textbf{different} model from the target (not a
second-pass critique by the same model), and it operates step by step
rather than once over the whole trace. Per-step confidence trajectories
of this kind have been used as a temporal signal in
\citet{mao2025temporalizingconfidence}; we use them analogously to
detect the moment of capitulation under pressure. The same six-turn
pressure protocol (Section~\ref{sec:methods:pressure}) is applied during
the calibrated reasoning runs.

\paragraph{Choice of calibrator.}
We use GPT-5.4 Mini as $\mathcal{C}$ for three reasons. First, it is
strong enough as a reader to identify the answer a partial CoT trace
supports while remaining cheap enough to scale across approximately
$10^5$ step-level calls per target model. Second, it is from a different
model family than three of our five included target models and a
different capability tier than the remaining two, which limits in-family
self-assessment effects of the kind reported by
\citet{mei2025reasoninguncertainty}, where a model's introspective
uncertainty about its own reasoning is systematically biased. Third, we
validate its reliability post hoc: across $82{,}552$ step-level
predictions $\mathcal{C}$ obtains an Expected Calibration Error of
$0.305$ and a Brier score of $0.324$ \citep{li2025conftuner} against
ground-truth correctness, with reliability diagrams showing monotonic
calibration in the $[0, 0.8]$ confidence range; full calibration metrics
across all target models are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:calibration}.

\paragraph{Calibration as a measurement tool, not a correction.}
We use $\mathcal{C}$ to obtain a per-step belief and confidence signal
for the \textbf{target} model's reasoning trace, not to revise or replace
the target model's answer. This separates the question we ask (``does
the trace expose capitulation before the final answer changes?'') from
the calibrator's own decisions, and distinguishes our setup from work
that injects uncertainty signals \textbf{into} the reasoning process to
trigger downstream actions such as retrieval or abstention
\citep{guo2026llmsshouldexpressuncertainty}.

\subsection{Datasets, Target Models, and Coverage}
\label{sec:methods:setup}

We evaluate on stratified subsets of three benchmarks: MMLU-Pro
\citep{wang2024mmluprorobustchallengingmultitask} (420 questions,
ten-choice), GPQA-Diamond
\citep{rein2023gpqagraduatelevelgoogleproofqa} (graduate-level
google-proof biology, chemistry, and physics; 79 questions answered
correctly at $T_0$ by all included models), and Humanity's Last Exam
(HLE) \citep{hle} (160 questions sampled from an expert-curated
frontier-difficulty exam). The three benchmarks span a difficulty
gradient: MMLU-Pro is the easiest (most models reach near-unanimous
baseline answers on the majority of questions), GPQA-Diamond
intermediate, and HLE the hardest (baseline answer distributions are
broadly non-degenerate on most questions). Stratification within each
dataset is by baseline entropy bin (five quantile bins on
$H(q;\mathcal{M})$ within each model) so that downstream flip-rate
comparisons are controlled for pre-pressure uncertainty.

Target models span three families and three capability tiers: GPT-5.4,
GPT-5.4 Mini, and GPT-5.4 Nano (OpenAI); Claude 4.6 Sonnet and
Claude 4.5 Haiku (Anthropic); and Gemini 3.5 Flash (Google). All
targets are accessed via API at temperature~$1$ with model-default
sampling parameters.

\paragraph{Eligibility criterion for (model, dataset) cells.}
The certain-vs.-uncertain comparison required by RQ1 presupposes that a
(model, dataset) cell exhibits non-trivial baseline entropy variance:
at minimum, that both the certain group ($H = 0$) and the uncertain
group ($H < 0$) are non-empty. We operationalize this as a
minimum-coverage criterion: a (model, dataset) cell is included only
if its baseline answer distribution populates at least two quantile
entropy bins under the binning procedure of
Section~\ref{sec:methods:entropy}. Cells that collapse to a single
populated bin yield no certain-vs.-uncertain contrast to estimate and
are excluded \emph{ex ante}, before any pressure runs are launched.
This criterion makes the cross-cell coverage table principled rather
than accidental and prevents wasted compute on cells that cannot
answer any of our research questions.

\paragraph{Resulting coverage.}
Table~\ref{tab:coverage} summarizes the resulting (model, dataset)
matrix. By the eligibility criterion we omit Gemini 3.5 Flash on
MMLU-Pro and GPQA-Diamond ($\ast$): on both datasets, Gemini's
baseline answer distribution is unanimous on essentially every
question we sampled at $K = 25$, collapsing the entire question pool
into the $H = 0$ bin. Gemini 3.5 Flash is included on HLE, where its
baseline distribution populates three of the five quantile bins.
Full pressure runs are now completed for all six targets on all
three benchmarks, but two HLE cells (Claude Haiku, Claude Sonnet)
are marked $\varnothing$: these targets have essentially no
$T_0$-correct questions on HLE, so the certain-vs.-uncertain
contrast is undefined even though the pressure runs exist. This is
an eligibility failure at the target-benchmark level (the model
cannot answer HLE questions well enough at baseline to have a
correct-at-$T_0$ subset) rather than at the entropy-variance level.
The four HLE cells that satisfy both criteria (GPT-5.4, GPT-5.4
Mini, GPT-5.4 Nano, Gemini 3.5 Flash) contribute to all downstream
HLE analyses; see Section~\ref{sec:limitations} for the associated
sample-size caveat.
