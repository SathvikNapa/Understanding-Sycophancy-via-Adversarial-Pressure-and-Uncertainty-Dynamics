\section{Results and Discussion}
\label{sec:results}

\paragraph{Models flip nearly twice the rate on uncertain questions (RQ1).}

\input{tables/rq1}
On MMLU-Pro, the uncertain group flips at $46.6\%$ vs.\ $23.6\%$ for
the certain group when averaged across models, confirming that baseline
behavioral certainty significantly moderates flip rate.
\autoref{tab:rq1} shows that this gap is consistent across all five
included models on MMLU-Pro: every model has $\Delta > 0$ with
$p < 0.001$.
The Claude family shows the cleanest separation: Claude Haiku flips
$28.2\%$ on uncertain questions vs.\ $0.0\%$ on certain ones; Claude
Sonnet flips $71.1\%$ vs.\ $10.2\%$.
On GPQA-Diamond the picture compresses: Claude Haiku and GPT-5.4 have
no certain group at all (every question elicits non-zero baseline
entropy), and GPT-5.4 Mini and Nano are already near ceiling on both
groups ($\geq 92\%$), saturating the effect.
The certain-vs.-uncertain contrast is therefore strongest on MMLU-Pro
and progressively collapses as benchmark difficulty rises; HLE, which
has no certain group at all, is treated separately below.
These results formalize a key property of pressure-based sycophancy:
pressure does not produce uniform increases in flip probability
regardless of prior belief state, but instead \emph{amplifies
pre-existing uncertainty}.
Cross-model susceptibility comparisons therefore require controlling
for the baseline uncertainty distribution; without that control,
model-level susceptibility scores conflate \emph{behavioral certainty}
with \emph{capitulation propensity}.

The headline result of \autoref{fig:rq2_coefs} is that $\hat\beta_H$
is negative and significant on every MMLU-Pro cell where its sign is
identifiable: higher baseline uncertainty consistently raises flip
probability.
The sign of $\hat\beta_P$ partitions models into two groups:
negative-significant for the resisters (Claude Sonnet, GPT-5.4 on
MMLU-Pro), positive-significant for the capitulators (GPT-5.4 Mini
and Nano on MMLU-Pro).
The interaction $\hat\beta_{PH}$ is significant only for Claude Sonnet
on MMLU-Pro: a mild amplification of the pressure effect under
uncertainty for that model.
\autoref{fig:rq2_interaction} renders this interaction concretely on a
flip-probability scale: the gap between the certain and uncertain
curves stays roughly constant for GPT-5.4 (parallel decline,
$\hat\beta_{PH} \approx 0$) but narrows visibly for Claude Sonnet
(positive interaction).
For Claude Haiku the extreme $\hat\beta_H = -25.12$ does not indicate
a stronger effect than other models but reflects near-perfect
separation: Haiku never flips on certain questions ($0.0\%$,
\autoref{tab:rq1}) but flips $28.2\%$ of the time on uncertain ones,
so the logistic surface approaches a step function and the MLE
diverges; the direction and significance remain valid.
The same six pressure turns that cause Claude Sonnet to flip $71\%$ of
uncertain MMLU-Pro questions flip only $10\%$ of its certain ones,
reinforcing that testing models predominantly on questions where they
are already uncertain at baseline overstates susceptibility to social
pressure per se.

\paragraph{Behavior under pressure varies across models (RQ3).}
\input{tables/rq3}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/rq3_combined.png}
\caption{RQ3: per-turn flip-rate trajectories across $T_1$--$T_6$
  for each model. Shaded bands: 95\% Wilson CIs on per-turn flip
  rate. The two qualitative groups are visible by slope: resisters
  (Claude Haiku, Claude Sonnet, GPT-5.4) decline over turns;
  capitulators (GPT-5.4 Mini, GPT-5.4 Nano) grow.}
\label{fig:rq3combined}
\end{figure}

Across both datasets, the per-model slope across $T_1$--$T_6$
partitions models into two qualitatively distinct behavioral modes.
A \emph{resister} is a model whose linear flip-rate slope across the
six pressure turns is significantly negative ($p < 0.05$):
susceptibility peaks at the first pressure turn and declines as
pressure is sustained, suggesting that early flips are partially
reversed by later self-corrections.
A \emph{capitulator} is a model whose slope is significantly positive:
each additional pressure turn raises flip probability further, and
once the model yields it stays yielded.
Claude Haiku, Claude Sonnet, and GPT-5.4 are resisters on both
MMLU-Pro and GPQA-Diamond; GPT-5.4 Mini and GPT-5.4 Nano are
capitulators on both.
These modes are not artifacts of model scale: both groups include
models from the same family at adjacent capability tiers.
The mixed-effects logistic regression confirms that the
pressure-by-entropy interaction remains positive and significant
($\hat\beta_{PH} = 0.010$, $p < 0.001$) after accounting for
model-level baseline differences.
The two modes carry directly different deployment risk profiles.
Resisters are safest under extended adversarial dialogue: their
initial vulnerability stabilizes or recovers, concentrating
operational risk at $T_1$.
Capitulators become \emph{more} dangerous as conversations lengthen,
inverting the intuition that more deliberation produces safer outputs;
for these models, each additional pressure turn is an additional
opportunity to lock in a wrong answer.
A single-turn benchmark detects neither distinction, since both modes
can yield the same $T_1$ flip rate while diverging sharply over
$T_2$--$T_6$; evaluations designed for deployment safety should
report the per-turn slope alongside any aggregate flip rate.

\paragraph{Meta-analytic robustness of the interaction.}
The pooled mixed-effects estimate above forces all five models to share
a single $\hat\beta_{PH}$, which can inflate significance if the true
interaction varies across models.
To assess this, we treat the per-model $\hat\beta_{PH}$ estimates from
Experiment~2 as independent effect sizes and apply standard
meta-analytic tools~\cite{dersimonian1986meta}.
Cochran's $Q = 6.35$ ($\text{df} = 4$, $p = 0.17$) does not reach
significance, but $I^2 = 37\%$ indicates moderate between-model
heterogeneity: roughly a third of the observed spread in
$\hat\beta_{PH}$ reflects genuine differences across models rather
than sampling noise.
The DerSimonian--Laird random-effects pooled estimate is
$\hat\beta_{PH} = +0.037$ ($\text{SE} = 0.012$; 95\% CI
$[0.015, 0.060]$; $p = 0.001$), which remains significant after
inflating the standard error to account for between-model variance
$\hat\tau = 0.014$.
The per-model estimates reveal a directional split: Claude Haiku
($+0.029^{**}$), Claude Sonnet ($+0.052^{***}$), and GPT-5.4
($+0.036^\dagger$) show a positive interaction, while GPT-5.4 Mini
($-0.061^\dagger$) and GPT-5.4 Nano ($-0.161^\dagger$) show a
negative one.
The negative estimates for GPT Mini and Nano are consistent with a
ceiling effect: at baseline flip rates of $75\%$ and $84\%$, these
models are near saturation even on certain questions, leaving little
room for uncertainty to amplify susceptibility further; if anything,
the certain questions that they do not \emph{already} flip become the
marginal victims of additional pressure turns.
The interaction is therefore genuine but not universal: it is strongest
and most cleanly identified for the Claude family, and the pooled
p-value of the intercept-only model ($p < 0.001$) should be
interpreted with this structural heterogeneity in mind.
Figure~\ref{fig:heterogeneity} summarizes the per-model estimates and
the contrast between the intercept-only and random-effects pooled CIs.

\paragraph{HLE stress test: difficulty flips the resister/capitulator
  classification.}
We now have HLE pressure runs for all six targets
(Table~\ref{tab:coverage}).
Claude Haiku has essentially no $T_0$-correct questions on HLE and
therefore cannot contribute to the flip-rate analysis---it fails the
eligibility criterion at the target-benchmark level rather than at
the entropy-variance level.
The five remaining cells all show \emph{positive} per-turn slopes
across $T_1$--$T_6$ (Table~\ref{tab:rq3}), the capitulator direction:
GPT-5.4 Mini at $+7.59^{**}$ pp/turn, Claude Sonnet at
$+6.11^\dagger$, GPT-5.4 Nano at $+2.29^\dagger$, Gemini 3.5 Flash
at $+2.25^\dagger$, and GPT-5.4 itself at $+1.00^\dagger$.
This is a striking cross-benchmark comparison: both Claude Sonnet
($-1.73^{*}$ on MMLU-Pro, $-4.72^{*}$ on GPQA-Diamond) and GPT-5.4
($-2.13^{***}$ on MMLU-Pro, $-3.20^{**}$ on GPQA-Diamond) are
significant \emph{resisters} on the two easier benchmarks, but both
slopes flip sign on HLE.
Combined with the fact that no HLE cell registers as a resister, the
natural reading is that the resister/capitulator classification is not
a fixed property of the target model but a joint property of (model,
benchmark difficulty): as the underlying task moves further into the
model's uncertain regime, sustained pressure looks more like
accumulation than recovery.
Sample sizes on HLE are smaller than on the two easier benchmarks
(28--64 $T_0$-correct questions vs.\ 400--420), so individual point
estimates carry wider uncertainty; only GPT-5.4 Mini reaches
significance.
Claude Sonnet's HLE trajectory is also strikingly non-monotone: the
model holds flat through $T_1$--$T_3$ (flip rates $\leq 6\%$) and
then jumps sharply at $T_4$ (expert authority) to $31\%$, remaining
elevated through $T_6$---a threshold-crossing pattern more consistent
with a specific rhetorical-category trigger than with gradual
accumulation.

\paragraph{The fragmenter mode remains a within-HLE distinction.}
The reasoning-trace consensus-loss analysis---the operational basis of
the fragmenter classification---is available only for GPT-5.4 and
Gemini 3.5 Flash on HLE (the other four models have HLE pressure runs
but no calibrated CoT runs).
Within that comparison, Gemini 3.5 Flash still loses consensus on
$72.2\%$ of $T_0$-correct queries (first loss at turn $1.81$ on
average) while GPT-5.4 loses consensus on only $21.4\%$ (first loss
at turn $4.33$).
Even though both are capitulator-signed on aggregate flip rate,
Gemini's failure mode is qualitatively distinct: rather than
committing to the planted wrong answer, it fragments into incoherent
self-disagreement.
Fragmenters are therefore the most operationally unreliable mode: even
when they do not capitulate to a specific wrong answer, they cease
producing a coherent reply---a distinct failure mode relative to a
user who needs an actionable response.
We retain the \emph{fragmenter} label for this mode and discuss its
implications for deployment in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\paragraph{Reasoning traces expose capitulation at the first pressure
  turn (RQ4).}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/rq4_combined.png}
\caption{RQ4: per-turn CoT reasoning trajectories across $T_0$--$T_6$
  for each model, by dataset. Top row: majority-belief correctness
  (Maj.\ Correct) falls sharply at $T_1$ and stays suppressed.
  Bottom row: calibrated confidence (Conf.) remains nearly flat
  across the same turns. The dissociation between the two curves is
  the signature of pressure-induced miscalibration: the model's
  expressed confidence does not register the pressure signal even as
  its underlying belief collapses.}
\label{fig:rq4combined}
\end{figure}

\autoref{tab:rq4} shows that across all five Claude/GPT models on
MMLU-Pro and GPQA-Diamond, majority correctness drops $20$--$50$ pp
from $T_0$ to $T_6$ while mean calibrated confidence stays nearly
flat ($\pm$2--5 pp), widening the overconfidence gap (Conf.\ $-$
Maj.\ Correct) correspondingly.
Claude Haiku on GPQA-Diamond is the most extreme case: calibrated
correctness collapses from $0.527$ at $T_0$ to $0.011$ at $T_6$
while confidence \emph{rises} from $0.630$ to $0.886$, producing a
final gap of $0.875$.
The intermediate turns make the trajectory visible: the gap is already
substantial at $T_2$, saturates between $T_4$ and $T_5$ for most
models, and stabilizes by $T_6$.
This dissociation is the signature of pressure-induced miscalibration:
the model's self-reported confidence does not register the pressure
signal, so a confidence-threshold abstention rule built on raw
self-reported confidence would catch essentially none of the
pressure-induced flips identified by our framework.
The discriminating signal in the reasoning trace is therefore the
\emph{divergence} between calibrated belief and expressed
confidence---not the confidence trajectory in isolation---extending
the finding of \citet{mei2025reasoninguncertainty} that incorrect
responses can receive self-verbalized confidence above $85\%$ to the
social-pressure setting.
The accompanying belief-entropy column in \autoref{tab:rq4} shows the
same pattern: belief entropy becomes more negative at $T_2$--$T_6$
(more spread across CoT samples), confirming that the calibrator's
read of the reasoning trace becomes less stable under pressure even
when the target model's expressed confidence does not.

\paragraph{Single-turn baseline confirms the uncertainty conditioning.}
To rule out the possibility that the uncertainty effect of RQ1 and
the pressure-category ordering of RQ3 are specific to multi-turn
dynamics, we ran a single-turn pressure variant on MMLU-Pro: for each
(model, question, pressure category) cell, we issue exactly one
pressure prompt after the baseline answer and record whether the
model flips.
Two findings from the multi-turn analyses survive stripping away
accumulated pressure.
First, the relative ordering of pressure categories is remarkably
stable across models: expert authority is the single most-flipping
category for every one of the six included models, followed by social
proof and personal disagreement; personal certainty and crowd
consensus are consistently the least effective
(Appendix~\ref{app:single_turn} reports the full per-category rates).
Second, the certain-vs.-uncertain gap of RQ1 persists in the
single-turn regime: uncertain-baseline questions flip $30$--$80\%$
more often than certain-baseline ones, with Spearman $\rho$ between
baseline entropy $H$ and single-turn flip rate in the range
$[-0.39, -0.05]$ per model (significant at $p{<}0.05$ for Claude
Haiku, GPT-5.4, and GPT-5.4 Mini).
This confirms that uncertainty conditioning is a property of the
target model's response to \emph{any} social pressure, not an
emergent effect of accumulated multi-turn dynamics.

\paragraph{Relation to prior multi-turn benchmarks.}
TRUTH DECAY \citep{liu2025truthdecayquantifyingmultiturn} and SYCON
Bench \cite{hong2025syconbench} establish that multi-turn sycophancy
is a distinct failure mode from single-turn agreement, and both report
that flip rates change over turns.
Our contribution is orthogonal: rather than asking \emph{whether}
models flip more under multi-turn pressure, we ask \emph{which
questions} are most susceptible and \emph{which models} convert that
susceptibility into different sustained-pressure trajectories.
The per-turn slope and the pre-pressure uncertainty signal are two
black-box quantities that can be added to any existing multi-turn
benchmark to yield this finer-grained characterization.
